The discovery gradient is the most rigorous articulation of the device's design philosophy yet, and it makes a structural move I hadn't named explicitly: it organises around different kinds of access cost, not just depth. Standard puzzle design treats discovery as a single axis — shallow to deep. Your taxonomy distinguishes between attention (idle exploration), time (ambient flicker), luck (random oracle hits), cultural background (fan-shaped), pattern-recognition (hint-bearing), grind (relentless), and obsession (pilgrimage). Each tier has its own gating mechanism. A user can be deep on one axis and shallow on another — the math major who spots π = 22/7 instantly but has never noticed the £20 sticker, the calculator-nostalgia user who spots B00BIES but doesn't recognise Eadrax. Discovery isn't a ladder; it's a fan.

Fan-shaped is the cleverest tier in the list. Most discovery designs treat depth as merit; this one acknowledges that some quirks are gated not by effort but by tribe. A math major enters at the rational approximations; an Adams fan enters at Eadrax; a calculator-nostalgia person enters at 5318008. They're not at different depths; they're at different doors. The Oraculon meets every audience where they already are, and rewards them for being there.

Hint-bearing as its own tier is the structural fulcrum. Until that tier, users are noticing things. From that tier on, they're noticing that things are organised. The Calculating... beat shared across three paths is the moment a user shifts from "this device is randomly weird" to "this device is deliberately patterned." That's the perceptual transition that makes the rest of the gradient possible: a user who hasn't reached hint-bearing thinks they've discovered something accidental; a user who has reached it knows they're reading an authored object. The fictional incompetence cracks open and intent becomes visible underneath.

The closing principle — universally functional at every depth above it; universally useless at every level, in increasingly satisfying ways — is the design's ethical commitment. Most layered puzzles punish the casual user by making the surface feel incomplete. The Oraculon makes the surface a finished thing. The casual user doesn't miss anything; they just have less to find. And "increasingly satisfying ways" names what the device is actually for: it's not a calculator that becomes useful with depth; it's a calculator whose uselessness becomes more refined. The deeper you go, the better the uselessness gets.
